Event-level comparison against ground truth.We match events by stable event ID and use the
ground-truth data as the denominator. The main event-level view is therefore a direct field-error
comparison rather than a detector-style precision/recall score. We report per-field error rates for
tick, division, dots, beam, and timeWarp, plus anany-fielderror rate that marks an event wrong
if any of the six core fields differ. Tick RMSE is reported separately in raw tick units. Following
the comparison script, tick matching uses a tolerance of ±1 tick for the event-level error rate, while
RMSE uses the raw difference.
Measure-level exactness and internal diagnostics.At the measure level, we report three exactmatch tiers against ground truth:perfect,voice match, andtick exact. A measure isperfectwhen the
voice partition matches ground truth and all core event fields match.Voice matchignores attribute
differences and only requires the canonicalized voice sets to agree.Tick exactrequires a voice
match and exact tick values for all events. In addition, we report internal structural diagnostics
from the evaluator in Appendix B, including average quality score, tick-twist, surplus time, and
broken-beam rate. These diagnostics do not compare against ground truth directly; they measure the
self-consistency of the produced result.
5.4 Configurations
Training configuration (BeadPicker).BeadPicker is a 16-layer Transformer encoder ( dmodel=128,
8 heads, dinner=512) with dropout 0.1, totaling about 3.24M parameters. We initialize all weight
matrices with Xavier-uniform initialization and a depth-scaled gain (g= 32 −1/2) to keep the 16-layer
stack stable at the start of training. Training uses multi-task supervision with a dominant weight on
the successor head; we mix large-scale rendered samples with recognition-feedback samples and
apply moderate augmentation to remain robust to layout variation and imperfect candidates. The full
hyperparameter list is provided in Appendix A.
Decoding configuration (BeadSolver).We evaluate two decoding budgets.Single-passBeadSolver
uses a fixed search budget and is meant to reflect the core capability of the model-guided solver
under a practical compute constraint.Multi-passBeadSolver runs multiple passes with escalating
budgets and returns the best solution found across passes; it is used to probe the upper bound of the
method when more compute is available. In our implementation, the passes share the same model
and evaluation function, and only differ in search quota; the multi-pass setting can also include a
lightweight pre-pass that assigns measure-level predispositions (e.g., estimated measure duration)
before search, which improves robustness on ambiguous measures.
5.5 Results
Our main comparison evaluates on the independent LilyPond test set against ground-truth topology
and reports both event-level and measure-level metrics (see Table 7).
Overall, greedy regulation struggles mainly on tick placement and voice organization: 30.65% of
scored events contain at least one field error, and only 50.71% of measures are fully correct. The
linear-equations baseline closes much of this gap, but it remains brittle on ambiguous timing and
time-warp structure. BeadSolver improves substantially once learned local guidance is combined
with search, reducing any-field event error to 5.91% and raising the perfect-measure rate to 83.00%.
