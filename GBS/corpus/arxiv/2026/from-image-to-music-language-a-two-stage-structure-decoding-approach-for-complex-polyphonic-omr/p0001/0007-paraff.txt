6 Discussion
6.1 Methodological Trade-Offs
Why search mattersThe flexibility of polyphonic staff notation makes reading and decoding
difficult in a very concrete way: quick visual intuition is often not enough to decide what is correct.
Many measures admit several locally plausible interpretations, and the right one only becomes clear
after checking global constraints such as voice continuity, measure duration, and rhythmic consistency.
In this sense, structure decoding is closer to logical analysis than to pattern matching.
This is similar to board games. A one-pass model can be surprisingly capable, but its ceiling is limited
when the decision requires exploring alternatives. When combined with Monte Carlo Tree Search,
the same model can reach a much higher level because it can branch, test, and back up evidence
instead of committing too early. Our solver follows the same principle: learned predictions provide
strong local guidance, while rule-based exploration and verification are used to resolve ambiguity.
For this reason, we believe that rule-driven exploration will remain a necessary component of an ideal
OMR system, whether the system is two-stage or end-to-end. Even with stronger models, complex
notation will continue to require explicit reasoning over constraints, especially in the long tail where
training data is sparse.
Why not reinforcement learningWe did not directly formulate the problem as reinforcement
learning for two main reasons. First, score regulation still lacks a universal rule standard for evaluating
solutions. We have designed several evaluation functions, but each comes with exception cases;
unlike board games, there is no single explicit rule judgment that remains reliable across the full
data distribution. Second, replacing these rules with a learned reward model would return us to
the same data bottleneck that the current method is designed to alleviate. In that sense, our present
search-based formulation is a practical compromise: it uses learned local priors where supervision is
available, while keeping global decision-making anchored by explicit constraints.
6.2 Issues remaining
Despite these benefits, our current system still has some limitations.
Weak global evaluation.Search only helps if we can reliably rank candidate structures. In our
implementation, the “value” of a partial or complete solution is dominated by hand-designed terms
with empirical weights. Some terms do incorporate model outputs, but the overall evaluator is still
relatively weak. In practice, we have seen exception cases where the correct solution receives a worse
score because it violates a heuristic assumption (e.g., informal rhythmic usage, slight engraving
