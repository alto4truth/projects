In experiments on an independent LilyPond test set, probability-guided search substantially improves
both tick accuracy and voice assignment over rule-based greedy decoding and a stronger linearequations baseline. This supports a practical view of OMR: when annotation budgets are limited,
combining learned predictors with rule-based exploration and evaluation can raise the ceiling of
recognition quality, while also producing structured data that can be fed back to train stronger models.
As a practical resource for future work, we are considering releasing the lilypond-topology
dataset used in this paper, including the LilyPond source files, engraved images, and the resulting
topology-cluster data. Looking ahead, we also plan to build a more complete benchmark that
evaluates a full OMR pipeline from images to a music-language representation, rather than only
isolated sub-tasks.
We also run an IMSLP 3 data mining project 4 that aims to convert open sheet-music images into
a usable symbolic music dataset. Our goal is to make this corpus practical for downstream tasks,
including symbolic music generation, algorithmic performance, and automatic transcription from
performances back to notation.
Several directions remain open. A stronger learned evaluator could reduce brittleness and improve
ranking among near-correct hypotheses. Efficiency is also important for unusually large measures,
where the search space becomes extreme. Tighter handling of upstream uncertainty in visually
sensitive semantics would make the full pipeline more robust. Finally, beyond data collection, we also
view RL-based finetuning of multimodal LLMs using these annotation agent rollouts as a promising
direction.
