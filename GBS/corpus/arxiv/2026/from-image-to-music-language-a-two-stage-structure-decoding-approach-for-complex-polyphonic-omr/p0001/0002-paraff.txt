Paraff-generated symbolic music is converted to LilyPond, engraved with randomized layout, and
then converted into measure-level event candidates with ground-truth structure. The second source is
recognition-feedback data extracted from corrected scores, which provides hard cases that match the
real distribution more closely. To emphasize high-quality corrected samples, we use three sampling
weights (1, 4, 100), which yields 918,930 effective weighted samples. We do not currently report
a dedicated cross-staff prevalence statistic for the training corpus. The generator and the feedback
portion both include cross-staff patterns, but their relative frequency may differ from the independent
LilyPond test set; we plan to include such stratified statistics in a future dataset release.
Three-stage quality filtering.Because the rendered source is produced by a long toolchain, we
apply quality checks at three points and drop any score that fails. The principle is to remove samples
that are structurally ill-defined, numerically invalid, or obviously inconsistent in space-time ordering,
rather than to overfit to a particular style. First, at the symbolic stage, we only keep measures that are
parseable and structurally complete, and we reject degenerate cases such as empty declared staves
or unclosed rhythmic constructs (e.g., broken beaming or invalid tuplet closure). Second, at the
engraving stage, we treat rendering failures and known unstable engraving patterns as hard filters (e.g.,
severe stem or beam anomalies reported by the engraver log), and we also drop samples that exceed a
runtime budget. Third, after converting the rendered result into event-topology clusters, we keep only
clusters that pass basic structural validity: they must contain real note/rest events, all coordinates and
ticks must be finite, and the implied time order should not strongly contradict horizontal layout (a
strong “time goes forward but moves left” pattern). During packaging we further remove clusters that
are too small to train on, fall outside target label ranges, or lack a valid supervision order.
Weighted sampling.The recognition-feedback portion is small by count but disproportionately
important: it contains hard measures that match production error patterns. We therefore use weighted
sampling so that corrected examples are seen more often during training, without losing the breadth
of the rendered corpus. Table 2 summarizes the three weight tiers and their effective contribution.
Split protocol.We split by score group to avoid leakage between training and validation. Following
Table 3, we use 9,697 groups (356,431 samples) for training and 198 groups (6,392 samples) for
validation; 100 groups are left unused by this split scheme.
