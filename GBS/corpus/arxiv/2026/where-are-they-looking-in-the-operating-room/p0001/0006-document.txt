To overcome these limitations, an alternative approach is to
predict where clinicians are looking by following their gaze
directions in a holistic view (e.g. ceiling-mounted cameras),
which is often referred to as gaze-following [28]. Unlike
traditional eye-tracking systems, gaze-following does not require additional devices, and has been extensively studied in
the general computer vision field [28, 29, 7, 8, 37, 36, 31],
along with several downstream applications such as intention
prediction [39], human-object interaction detection [40], gaze
communication detection [13, 14], and human-robot interaction [32, 18]. However, gaze-following and its applications in
the OR still remain unexplored.
