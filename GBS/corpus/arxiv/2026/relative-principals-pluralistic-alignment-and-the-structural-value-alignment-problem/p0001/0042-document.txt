4 Alignment with Pluralistic Values
A fundamental challenge emerges from the structural account of value alignment: namely, that alignment cannot
be conceived monolithically. Current technical approaches to alignment—most notably, RLHF—are primarily
designed to align models with the average preferences of humans. While this strategy is often framed as
“democratic”, it effectively erases diversity by smoothing over legitimate differences among individual and group
values [3, 70, 71]. This observation should be understood as a diagnostic claim about prevailing training and
deployment incentives rather than a dismissal of emerging pluralistic alignment research. In principle, feedback
aggregation methods can be designed to preserve disagreement, represent minority viewpoints, or enable
deliberative synthesis among competing perspectives. In practice, however, standard deployment pipelines—
especially those optimised for scalability, consistency, and product usability—tend to reward the production of a
single coherent response distribution, which implicitly encourages the smoothing or aggregation of divergent
